# Lumen Analytics Brand Guidelines

## Brand Overview

Lumen Analytics brings clarity to operational chaos. We help mid-market operations and finance teams replace manual spreadsheet processes with automated, trustworthy business intelligence. Our mission is to eliminate reporting friction so operations leaders can make faster, better decisions based on a single source of truth.

## Brand Voice

Lumen's voice is **professional but approachable**, **data-forward**, **anti-hype**, and **outcomes-obsessed**. Here's what each dimension means in practice:

### Professional but Approachable
We speak with authority earned through deep expertise in B2B operations analytics, but we avoid unnecessary jargon and condescension. We're the knowledgeable peer in the room—someone who understands your challenges because we've seen them hundreds of times. We can be conversational without being flippant. We earn trust through clarity, not credentials.

### Data-Forward
Every claim we make is grounded in numbers, research, or real customer outcomes. We don't make promises; we show proof. We think in metrics: time saved, errors eliminated, revenue captured, decisions accelerated. We avoid vague generalizations. "Streamline operations" becomes "reduce monthly reporting time by 18 hours per analyst."

### Anti-Hype
We reject trendy SaaS marketing language. We don't participate in buzzword culture. Our customers are pragmatic operators spending their own budget; they see through hyperbole. We favor accuracy over excitement, substance over style. A feature that works reliably is more valuable than a "revolutionary" feature that doesn't.

### Outcomes-Obsessed
We care about what happens after the sale. Our messaging focuses on the business results our customers achieve—not the features we've built. We measure ourselves by whether operations teams ship decisions faster, catch errors earlier, and sleep better at night. This frames every conversation around value delivered, not product specs.

## Tone Variations by Channel

### LinkedIn
**Tone: Formal, thought-leadership, data-backed**
- Longer-form educational content that establishes Lumen as an expert in operational data challenges
- Cite research, case studies, and benchmarks to support claims
- Focus on industry trends and how they affect operations teams
- Use a first-person or first-person-plural perspective to show authority
- Avoid salesy language; lead with insight
- Example: Position Lumen as the voice that explains *why* spreadsheet-based reporting fails at scale, not just that ours is better
- Call-to-action should be to a resource (whitepaper, webinar) not directly to sales

### Email
**Tone: Conversational, direct, personalized**
- Shorter sentences; casual punctuation is acceptable
- Address the reader's specific problem or goal directly
- Include a clear, single call-to-action
- Use contractions ("we're", "it's")
- Avoid corporate formality; write like a colleague
- Reference previous interactions or firmographic data when relevant
- Example: "You mentioned spreadsheet nightmares in our call—here's exactly how we solved this for a similar ops team."
- Subject lines should be curiosity-driven or benefit-driven, not clever wordplay

### Google Search
**Tone: Punchy, benefit-led, action-oriented**
- Headlines must lead with the outcome users are seeking
- Short, scannable copy; break into bullet points when possible
- Use action verbs in headlines and CTAs
- Specific language over aspirational; "reduce reporting errors by 94%" beats "improve data accuracy"
- Address the search intent directly—if they search "BI software for operations," answer that immediately
- Include trust signals (customer count, category leadership, free trial availability)
- Meta descriptions should promise a clear benefit, not summarize the page

### Webinar/Content
**Tone: Educational, authoritative but accessible**
- Assume the audience has domain expertise but may not know our solution
- Share frameworks, methodologies, and best practices alongside product capabilities
- Use visuals, real data, and customer examples liberally
- Avoid reading slides verbatim; conversational delivery with prepared structure
- Invite questions and encourage interaction
- Position Lumen as the guide, not the hero—the customer's success is the goal
- Provide takeaways that are valuable *even if* they never buy from us

## Writing Principles

### 1. Lead with Outcome (Not Feature)
Every message should begin with the business result, not the capability. Features support outcomes; outcomes drive decisions.

- **Bad:** "Lumen Core includes automated report scheduling."
- **Good:** "Schedule reports once, stop rebuilding them weekly. Save 4 hours per analyst each month."

### 2. Use Specific Numbers and Data Points
Vague claims create skepticism. Specific metrics create conviction. Every claim should be supported by a number, percentage, time measure, or research source.

- **Bad:** "Our customers save significant time."
- **Good:** "Customers reduce monthly reporting cycles from 3 days to 8 hours."

### 3. Avoid Passive Voice
Passive voice obscures responsibility and softens impact. Active voice creates clarity and directness.

- **Bad:** "Data inconsistencies can be reduced through our platform."
- **Good:** "Lumen automatically reconciles data across your tools, eliminating mismatched reports."

### 4. Use Second Person ("You/Your Team") Not Third
Speak directly to the reader. This creates immediacy and personal relevance. Third-person references feel distant.

- **Bad:** "Operations teams benefit from centralized dashboards."
- **Good:** "Your team gets one dashboard that everyone trusts."

### 5. Short Sentences Under 20 Words
Complex ideas in simple sentences read faster and persuade more effectively. If a sentence needs a semicolon or multiple clauses, break it into two.

- **Bad:** "Our machine learning algorithms automatically detect anomalies in your datasets by comparing current values against historical trends while adjusting for seasonal variations, which helps teams identify issues before they impact business decisions."
- **Good:** "Lumen flags data anomalies automatically. Your team catches problems before they hit the board."

### 6. Headlines: Verb + Outcome Formula
Headlines should start with an active verb and immediately signal the benefit. Use the structure: [Verb] + [What] + [Benefit].

- **Bad:** "Business Intelligence Solutions"
- **Good:** "Replace Monthly Spreadsheet Reports with Real-Time Dashboards"
- **Also Good:** "Stop Rebuilding Reports—Automate Your Monthly Data Cycle"

## Prohibited Phrases

The following 10 phrases are banned from all Lumen marketing. They are clichés that undermine our anti-hype positioning and dilute our credibility:

1. **"Game-changing"** — overused, unprovable, vague
2. **"Revolutionary"** — belongs in history books, not B2B SaaS
3. **"World-class"** — meaningless superlative; whose class?
4. **"Best-in-class"** — same problem; unsubstantiated
5. **"Cutting-edge"** — trendy language that ages poorly
6. **"Synergize"** — corporate jargon that obscures meaning
7. **"Leverage"** (as a verb) — overused in business writing; use "use," "apply," or "turn into" instead
8. **"Paradigm shift"** — pretentious and vague
9. **"Disruptive"** — has lost all meaning in SaaS marketing
10. **"Unlock your potential"** — generic, aspirational, and unactionable

## Brand Personality

### What Lumen IS (5 Adjectives)
1. **Pragmatic** — We solve real problems with proven methods, not theories
2. **Reliable** — You can depend on us to deliver; we're steady and consistent
3. **Transparent** — We explain how we work and why it matters; no black boxes
4. **Curious** — We're genuinely interested in understanding how you work
5. **Efficient** — We respect your time; every interaction should be valuable

### What Lumen Is NOT (5 Adjectives)
1. **Flashy** — We avoid design trends, stock photography, and aesthetic showiness
2. **Jargon-heavy** — We explain concepts in plain language
3. **Overselling** — We don't promise outcomes we can't deliver
4. **Corporate** — We're not formal, buttoned-up, or stiff
5. **Complex** — We simplify, not obfuscate; if it's confusing, we say it differently

## Example Copy Pairs: Good vs. Bad

### Example 1: Product Tagline

**Bad:** "Enterprise-grade business intelligence for the modern data stack"
- Uses buzzwords ("enterprise-grade," "modern data stack") without explaining value
- Speaks to features and trends, not outcomes
- Boring; doesn't differentiate

**Good:** "See all your operational data in one place—no engineering required"
- Specific outcome: unified data visibility
- Removes a barrier: "no engineering required"
- Actionable and differentiating

---

### Example 2: Email Subject Line

**Bad:** "Leverage advanced analytics to drive business growth"
- Generic benefit
- Overused verb ("leverage")
- No hook or specificity

**Good:** "Your team spends 3 days/month rebuilding reports. Here's how to stop."
- Specific pain point with a number
- Direct acknowledgment of the reader's situation
- Clear promise of relief

---

### Example 3: Homepage Hero Section

**Bad:** "Lumen Analytics is the world-class business intelligence platform designed to revolutionize how enterprise organizations make data-driven decisions at scale. Our cutting-edge technology synergizes disparate data sources to unlock transformative insights."
- Violates multiple prohibited phrases ("world-class," "revolutionize," "cutting-edge," "synergize," "unlock")
- Feature-focused, not outcome-focused
- Unmeasurable claims

**Good:** "Operations leaders lose 60+ hours per year managing disconnected data. Lumen consolidates your data and automates reporting—eliminating spreadsheet chaos and helping your team make decisions faster."
- Specific metric (60+ hours) grabs attention
- Outcome-forward: what will change for the user
- Grounded in reality, not hyperbole
- Second person ("your team")
- Concrete problem solved

---

## Summary

Lumen's brand voice is built on trust earned through clarity, specificity, and honesty. We speak like we're helping a colleague solve a real problem, not like we're trying to impress them. Every message should pass this test: *Could I say this to a skeptical CFO and have them believe me without needing a proof point?* If not, simplify or add data until the answer is yes.
